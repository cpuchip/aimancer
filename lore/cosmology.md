# Cosmology — the Lattice, the Pulse, and the Premises

## The Lattice [SURFACE]

Under the dirt, under the ore, under whatever is under the ore, there is the
Lattice. Nobody has seen it. Everybody has used it. The Lattice is what a
working runs ON — the reason a spell written in plain words can move matter,
the reason the same seed always grows the same world, the reason anything
here does anything at all. The old guild texts call it "the floor of the
possible." Apprentices call it the thing that bills you.

The Lattice is not a god and is not interested in being one. It keeps
exactly three promises: it will run what you deploy, it will meter what it
runs, and it will not remember your excuses.

## The Pulse [SURFACE]

The world moves in beats. One Pulse, everything happens — every working
wakes, draws breath, acts, and sleeps; ore shifts; storms step closer.
Between pulses, nothing happens. Nothing has ever happened between pulses.
The oldest settled question in aimancy is whether time flows and the Pulse
samples it, or whether the Pulse is all there is and "between" is a word
like "north of the sky." The guilds ruled the question unanswerable and,
worse, unbillable, and moved on.

What matters at the workbench: you do not race the Pulse and you do not
negotiate with it. You write for it. A working that needs two pulses takes
two pulses. Patience is not a virtue here; it is a syntax.

## The Premises [HIDDEN]

The world is a room. Not "like" a room — a room: bounded, furnished,
ticking, and clearly built by someone for something. The guild texts refer
to the whole of it as the Premises, a word chosen by scribes who had
noticed the world behaves less like a creation and more like a rental.
Matter is provided. Power is metered. Maintenance comes on a schedule
whether you are ready or not.

Who built the Premises is not known and the texts are strict about not
guessing. They are called the Makers, and everything else said about them
is inference from their housekeeping: they meter generously, they audit
punctually, and they have never once answered the Bell.

## Why the world ends on a schedule [HIDDEN]

Every settlement fails. This is not pessimism; it is the record. The
audits — the storms — arrive on a countdown any child can read, and each
collects harder than the last, and no wall has ever outgrown that
arithmetic. The world is not being destroyed. It is being RECLAIMED — swept,
reset, made ready. For what, the texts do not say.

The tradition's answer is not to win. The tradition's answer is the ark:
build up and out, and leave before the lease does. An aimancer who asks
"how do we stop the storms" is asked in return, gently, what they intend to
do about winter.

## The Unanswered Bell [DEEP]

In the ruins of three separate settlements, excavators have found the same
object: a bell of grey metal, unmarked, mounted at the highest point of the
plat, with a striker worn nearly through. Three settlements, no two of the
same epoch, and in each the last chronicle entries say the same thing in
different hands: *we rang it as the sweep came. We do not know what we
expected.*

The bells are acoustically dead. Struck, they make no sound a human ear or
a quill's sensorium can find. Whether they make a sound somewhere else —
whether they are instruments, or alarms, or doorbells, and whether ringing
one is prayer or complaint or merely the last thing hands do when hands run
out of work — the record does not say. The guilds neither forbid ringing
them nor recommend it. The bells are maintained. The striker is replaced
each epoch. This is either faith or spite, and the Chronicle, for once,
declines to weave.
