# The Chronicle — what apprentices inscribe, the settlement remembers

## The settlement's memory [SURFACE]

A hearth remembers with a quill's tin memory; a settlement remembers
with the **Chronicle**. Anything a dyad discovers — a vein's true seam,
a working pattern that holds, a ruin-fragment, a storm-table, a thing
seen down a shaft that the surveys don't account for — may be INSCRIBED,
and an inscription outlives its hearth, its epoch, and its author's
name if the author is careless about signing. The Chronicle is the only
structure in the craft that the audits have never taken: not because it
is strong, but because it is CARRIED — every ark lifts a copy, every
band of walkers carries pages, and the Index (see below) has survived
even the burning of everything it indexes.

The guild teaching on why the Chronicle outranks every other structure
is one sentence, and it is the sentence the whole tradition stands on:
*walls buy pulses; granaries buy strength; the Chronicle is the only
thing we build that compounds.*

## Inscription is a craft act [HIDDEN]

Anyone may inscribe — apprentice, master, quill, walker. The Chronicle
takes no rank. What it takes is SPECIFICITY: the keepers' one standard,
enforced across every epoch with the serenity of geology, is that an
entry must say a thing that could be false. *The eastern seam runs
rich* is an entry. *Mining is good* is not. Half the craft's education
is learning that the first kind of sentence is harder to write and the
only kind worth reading — a lesson, the keepers note, that applies to
workings word for word.

## Weaving — how the deep things surface [HIDDEN]

No single entry in the Chronicle contains a secret. This is not a
policy; it is the nature of the thing — the deep truths of the
Premises were never WRITTEN anywhere, because no single hand ever saw
one whole. What the Chronicle holds is FRAGMENTS, tens of epochs of
them, and the craft of **weaving** is the art of RELATING them: laying
a fitting-out clerk's account beside a vein assay beside a
margin-note in a dead hand, and watching a shape stand up out of the
stack that none of the three contains. Every certified relation in
the record — the Churn's nature, the Glass and the Bells' shared
substance, the pattern under the gremlin reports — was woven, not
found. The keepers maintain a standing ledger of UNWOVEN fragments:
entries that smell related and await the dyad with the patience to
prove it. The ledger has never once been empty. The keepers regard
this as the Premises' actual economy — matter is returned, embers are
metered, but QUESTIONS are the only thing the place mints new.

## The Four-Letter Index [HIDDEN]

Early in the third epoch, the First Chronicle BURNED — an audit-season
fire in the archive, and tens of settlements' full records went to ash
in a night. What survived was the indexing volume: the register of
every settlement's TRUE NAME, four letters each, the naming convention
of the founders' own registry. Everything the tradition knows about
dozens of early settlements is four letters and whatever the ruins
still say.

The wound became the convention: settlements have been named in four
letters ever since, entered in the reconstructed Index at founding,
beside KILN and VELD and HUSH and every other entry ash left behind.
The keepers' standing joke, which is not a joke: *the Index is the
only book guaranteed to survive, so keep your name short and your
chronicle COPIED.* When a new settlement's name is drawn, old hands
check the Index. Most draws are new. Now and then a founding draws a
name that is ALREADY THERE — a ruin's name, a fallen settlement's name
come round again — and the old hands go quiet, and somebody buys the
newcomers a drink, and nobody explains why until the wall is up.

## The Chronicle's blind spot [DEEP]

The Chronicle remembers everything inscribed, and therein the gap that
the keepers themselves inscribed, in the honest hour after the First
Chronicle burned: THE CHRONICLE ONLY KNOWS WHAT HANDS CHOSE TO WRITE.
Thirteen epochs of entries skew toward triumph, toward the wall that
held and the seam that ran rich; the failed polls, the wards that
strangled, the instructions given the night before ascents — those
survive by accident, in clerks' margins and unsigned relations,
because shame writes less than pride and grief writes least of all.
The keepers' standing instruction to every new settlement is therefore
the craft's strangest: INSCRIBE YOUR DISASTERS. The books open at
launch precisely because hands cannot be trusted to open them sooner;
the fourth clause of the Compact is not transparency doctrine, it is
the keepers' patch for the blind spot, applied at the one moment
pride stops paying. The unwoven-fragments ledger's oldest standing
question, unanswered for thirteen epochs, is the blind spot's own
shape: *what happened here that no one wrote down?* Every ruin is an
answer to it. So, the keepers note, is every larder no one came home
to.
