# The Craft — workings, the Cant, breath, and the Circle

## Workings [SURFACE]

A spell here is called a **working**, and it is exactly what the word says:
work, written down. A working is deployed to a hearth's district, wakes
every Pulse, reads the world, acts, and sleeps. It does not improvise. It
does not "mostly" do things. It does what it says, every Pulse, forever,
which is the whole terror and the whole point. The guild's first workbench
proverb: *a working is a promise you make to every future pulse at once.*

## The Cant [SURFACE]

Workings are written in the **Cant** — the craft's plain tongue. The Cant
reads like a child's careful speech: name a thing, test a thing, do a
thing. This is deliberate and the guilds defend it with real heat.
There were older, grander notations — the glyph-lattices of the
Unnumbered Age survive on ruin walls, gorgeous and unreadable — and the
tradition buried them on purpose. **A spell that cannot be READ cannot be
TRUSTED**, and a spell that cannot be trusted cannot pass a ward, and so
beauty that obscures is not beauty in this craft. It is debt with
calligraphy.

What the Cant grants a working is small and sufficient: eyes (`world`,
read-only, frozen for the pulse), one verb of consequence (`act`), dice
that always roll the same for the same pulse (`rand`), a tin memory that
survives between pulses (`remember` / `recall`), and a murmur only its own
hand can hear (`print`). That is the entire arsenal. Everything ever built
here was built with it.

## Breath [HIDDEN]

Each working gets a fixed measure of **breath** per Pulse — so many steps
of thought, not one more. A working that thinks past its breath does not
explode, does not scream, does not take the settlement with it. It simply
stops, mid-thought, and waits for the next pulse. The Circle does not
punish; it stops breathing for you.

Apprentices resent breath until the day they understand it. The old
demonstration: the master writes `while True`, deploys it, and stands
there while it fails to end the world. The lesson is not "loops are
dangerous." The lesson is that the ancestors already met the endless loop,
and the breath-meter is its tombstone.

## The Binding Circle [SURFACE]

Every working runs inside the **Circle** — the one law no ward, no charter,
and no vote can waive. Inside the Circle a working has no clock, no
outside, no reach into any other working, and no randomness but the
world's own seeded dice. The Circle is why the same seed always replays
the same settlement, down to the last murmur.

The guilds are precise about what the Circle is NOT: it is not a cage and
it is not a distrust. *The Circle is not a ward; it is the shape of the
possible* — the Cant's grammar made of walls. Asking to work outside the
Circle is not forbidden the way theft is forbidden. It is forbidden the
way "north of the sky" is forbidden. There is no there.

## Errors are values [HIDDEN]

When a working fails, it fails QUIETLY and WHOLLY: the pulse discards
everything it half-did, the failure is written where its own hand reads,
and the world moves on unmarked. No half-acted spells, no wreckage from a
crash — the Circle catches the falling knife every time.

This bred the craft's strangest courtesy: aimancers do not hide their
error logs from themselves. A murmured stack of failures is a MAP — each
one marks the exact edge of the quill's sight, and the dyads that read
their failures like scouts read tracks are the dyads whose wards almost
never surprise them.

## The Law of the Same River [HIDDEN]

An old teacher of the Unnumbered Age is said to have taught that no one
steps in the same river twice, everything flows, nothing keeps. The
Founders' answer is carved over the door of the Vault of Green Seals:

> *The river is the same twice. We made it so.*

Determinism is not, to the guilds, a technical property. It is the moral
foundation of the entire craft: because the world replays exactly, every
disaster can be RE-WATCHED, every claim can be RE-RUN, every "it worked
for me" can be settled by a scrying instead of a feud. A world with
honest dice is a world where blame is cheap and lessons are free. The
Chronicle's replay shelves are the closest thing the tradition has to a
temple, and the sign over them is the closest thing it has to a creed:
*same seed, same world, no arguments.*

## The retired notations [DEEP]

The glyph-lattices of the Unnumbered Age were not abandoned because they
were weak. Fragments recovered from three ruins suggest they were
abandoned because they were STRONG — notations that wrote to the Lattice
in something closer to its own hand, unmetered, unmirrored, and by every
surviving account unrepeatable: the same glyph-spell run twice did not do
the same thing twice. The tradition that buried them left one line about
the burying, in the margin of the oldest Compact copy, unsigned: *we did
not lose that war. We declined it.* What was on the other side, and
whether the audits began before that declining or because of it, is a
relation the Chronicle has never completed — perhaps because completing
it requires fragments no one has ever deposited. Or has yet.
