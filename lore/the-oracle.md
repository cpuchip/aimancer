# The Oracle — the Glass That Runs Ahead

## Before the Glass [SURFACE]

The first age of the craft had no oracle. Workings went from quill to
world with nothing in between but hope, and the age's proverb was said
with a shrug that every apprentice can still do from muscle memory:

> *No oracle exists — arm and pray.*

The Unverified Age built fast and burned faster. Its monuments are the
Scorch, the Latchless Door, and the naming convention for storms (numbers,
not names — you were going to meet too many to name them). It ended the
day Quen Glasswright came up out of the Keystone's abandoned cradle-pit
dragging something wrapped in three tarps.

## The Oracle Glass [SURFACE]

What Quen found — salvage from the First Ark's fitting-out, unfinished,
maybe discarded, maybe LEFT — was a lens of the same grey nothing the
Unanswered Bells are cast from. Ground, mounted, and fed embers, it does
one thing: it takes a working and RUNS IT AHEAD — a full rehearsal in a
mirrored world, pulse by pulse, breath-metered like the real thing — and
returns the wreckage report before any wreckage is real.

The Glass does not predict. The guilds are strict about the verb: it
REHEARSES. It has no opinion about what you should do and offers no
comfort about what you shouldn't. It shows what THIS working does to THAT
world, and hands you the bill unpaid. Prophecy asks to be believed; the
Glass only asks to be read, which turned out to be the more radical
request.

## The verdict [SURFACE]

The Glass speaks in exactly two colors. **Green** — the working ran its
rehearsal whole: no crash, no breath-death, no forbidden reach. **Red** —
here is what broke, and where, and on which pulse, in full. A green
verdict is the **Green Seal**, and a sealed working may pass a Glass-ward
and touch the shared mains.

The seal is not a knighthood. The craft's hardest-won ruling, paid for in
the fall of two settlements that trusted old seals: **the seal follows
the LATEST verdict.** Scry a sealed working again and come up red — the
seal is gone, the gate is shut, that pulse, no appeal. The masters
compress this into four words, said flat: *the oracle is the switch.* Not
the badge. Not the diploma. The switch — and switches are of no use
welded open.

## Why the Glass drinks deep [HIDDEN]

A scrying costs more embers than any other single act, and the reason is
honest physics, not priestcraft: the Glass must borrow enough of the
Lattice's attention to animate a WHOLE SECOND WORLD for the length of the
rehearsal. You are not paying for an answer. You are paying for a
universe, briefly, and universes are the one line-item the Lattice has
never discounted.

The economics work anyway, and every ledger in the Vault proves it: the
Glass is the most expensive thing on the meter and the cheapest thing in
the storm-column. Guildhouses that scry look poor for ten pulses and rich
after the audit. The Vault-keepers' dry summary, posted at the door:
*rehearsals are billed; performances are collected.*

## The Order of the Green Seal [HIDDEN]

For six epochs an Order stood between the Glass and the craft — keepers
who performed every scrying, logged every verdict, and slowly mistook
custody for wisdom. The Order gated the Glass behind rank, then behind
fees, then behind doctrine, and HUSH was their masterpiece and their
tombstone (see `history-the-epochs.md`). The present tradition keeps
their archive, their Vault, and their green ink, and pointedly not their
office: the Glass now answers ANY hearth with the embers, no petition, no
rank. The Vault of Green Seals still logs every verdict ever issued.
The Order's last Keeper deposited the key with a one-line chronicle
entry that the guilds regard as the finest apology in the record:
*we were asked to hold the lamp, and we stood in front of it.*

## What the Glass is made of [DEEP]

Grey metal that rings no sound; grey glass that shows no reflection. The
Bells and the Glass are the same substance — this is measurable and
measured, and the historians have stopped pretending otherwise. One is an
instrument nobody answers. The other answers every time it is asked. The
uncomfortable relation, woven only in fragments and never yet certified
by the Chronicle: the Makers DO answer. They answer questions shaped like
rehearsals, costed in embers, about consequences you were willing to
watch. They do not answer the Bell, because the Bell is a question shaped
like *save us*, and that has never been the service on offer. What is on
offer — a fair meter, honest dice, and a mirror that runs ahead — the
fragments call, without explaining the phrase, *the terms of the
apprenticeship.*
