# The Rite of Four — shelter the work, the body, the stranger; then go

## The rite [SURFACE]

Every settlement in the tradition builds the same four things, in the
same order, and has since the Keystone's survivors wrote the order down
with the ash still on their hands: **Wall, Granary, Beacon, Ark.** The
rite is usually taught to children as a sentence before it is taught as
a building code:

> *Shelter the work. Shelter the body. Shelter the stranger. Then go.*

Later structures stay locked until the earlier milestone stands — not by
decree but by the plain masonry of the thing: a granary raised before a
wall is storm-food, a beacon lit before a granary is an invitation to
starve together, an ark begun before all three is the Plumbline with
extra steps. The rite is the tradition's entire strategic doctrine
compressed to a build order, and settlements that improvised on it fill
a respectable shelf of the Index's ruins.

## The Wall — why it absorbs for everyone [SURFACE]

The wall is the first thing built and the strangest thing in the craft,
and its strangeness is easy to miss: the wall does not defend a hearth.
It cannot. There has never been a private wall on any plat in the
record — the wall rings the WHOLE settlement or it is not a wall, and
every part sworn into it thickens it for every hearth alike, including
the hearth that contributed nothing, including the newcomer who drops in
one pulse before the storm.

This is not generosity. It is the load-path. A wall is COMMUNAL
COHERENCE made stone: each part that passes the gate is verified work,
witnessed and sealed and reconciled, and reconciled work is the only
substance the Audit respects (see `storms-and-the-audit.md`). A wall
built of sealed parts presents the storm with a single, settled,
collective account — and the storm takes what the account absorbs and
spills the remainder on EVERYONE, because the settlement was always one
entry in the books, however many hearths signed it. The guilds put it
without sentiment: *you do not have a wall. The settlement has a wall,
and you have a settlement.*

Contributions never stop mattering: storms drain the wall and further
sworn parts heal it, up to the masonry's limit. A wall is not a
milestone that stays passed. It is a promise that stays kept, or
doesn't.

## The Granary [HIDDEN]

The granary is the settlement's shared stomach: food stored collectively,
drawn on collectively, and the precondition for every mouth the
settlement hopes to add. The granary sermons are the tradition's most
repetitive and its most necessary — windfalls build arks, wages feed
builders — because ore is exciting and food is not, and the Index's
ruins include more starved settlements than breached ones. The masters
note which failure the young guard against, and which one gets them.

## The Beacon and the survivors [HIDDEN]

The beacon is the rite's third clause made literal: a light raised for
whoever is out there. And there is always someone out there — walkers
between plats, out of settlements the audits already closed, steering
between storms by count and hope. A lit beacon brings them in, a stocked
granary keeps them, and every pair of survivors sheltered RAISES THE
WHOLE SETTLEMENT'S CRAFT: more workings deployable at every hearth, the
capacity of the place growing with its mercy.

The mechanism is not magic and the guilds resist prettifying it: each
pair of hands that walks in REMEMBERS A WORKING — carries craft from a
fallen settlement, patterns and load-words and one more way to phrase a
miner's loop. The beacon is the only structure in the rite that pays out
in SKILL, and the tradition's judgment on that design is carved on
beacon-bases across the record: *the stranger is not a mouth. The
stranger is a library that walks.*

## Why the rite ends with leaving [HIDDEN]

Wall, granary, beacon — every clause of the rite shelters something, and
then the last clause abandons all three. The apparent contradiction is
the tradition's deepest teaching, and the masters let apprentices sit
with it unresolved for as long as it takes: the wall will not hold
forever, and was never meant to; it is the rite's way of buying TIME.
The granary buys STRENGTH. The beacon buys HANDS AND MEMORY. And the
ark spends all three on the only purchase that outlasts an audit —
LEAVING WELL. The rite is not a fortress doctrine that fails at the
end. It is a departure checklist that begins with kindness. The
Keystone's survivors, who wrote it, put the resolution in the margin of
the original tablet, where it remains: *we do not build to stay. We
build so that going is a harvest, not an escape.*
