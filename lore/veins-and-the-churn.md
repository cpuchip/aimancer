# Veins and the Churn — why matter surfaces on a schedule

## The veins [SURFACE]

Ore does not lie waiting in this world; it ARRIVES. A vein surfaces —
breaks the plat like a slow whale — runs rich for a while at whatever
rate its seam allows, and then runs DRY, wholly and forever, and your
miners stand there holding the silence until the next one comes. New
veins keep coming, on a cadence every settlement learns to feel in its
teeth, until they don't. The supply is a schedule, not a landscape.

The practical craft is therefore RE-TARGETING: a miner bound to a dead
vein is a working spending breath on a memory. The templates the guild
hands every newcomer mine *the richest live vein*, not *the vein I
started with*, and the difference between those two phrases has fed
more settlements than any other line of Cant ever written.

## Farming needs no vein [HIDDEN]

Food is the counter-truth: fields are slow and never run dry. The
guilds pair the two facts as the settlement's fundamental rhythm — ore
is a WINDFALL and food is a WAGE — and every granary sermon since the
Bellows epoch has been some arrangement of that sentence. Windfalls
build arks. Wages keep the builders alive to finish them.

## The Churn [HIDDEN]

Why does matter surface on a schedule? Because the world is not making
ore. It is RETURNING it. Beneath the plat, the strata turn — slowly,
massively, the way a sleeper turns — and the guild name for that turning
is the **Churn**. What the Churn carries up is not raw stone: assayers
have pulled worked metal from every vein ever tapped. Rivets. Hinge-
pins. A gear tooth, polished by something other than geology. Melted
lattice-work in shapes no current guild casts. The veins are STRATA —
the pressed and folded remains of everything the audits ever swept, of
every settlement that stood on this plat before yours — and the Churn
hands them back up, seam by seam, on the Premises' own unhurried
timetable.

Every part you craft is therefore twice-made. Every wall is built of
older walls. The miners' toast, older than any surviving guildhouse,
raised at every new vein's surfacing: *to the previous management.*

## Mining the ancestors [HIDDEN]

The tradition has made its peace with what the veins are, and the peace
is warmer than an outsider expects. To mine a vein is not grave-robbery;
it is INHERITANCE — the only form of it the audits permit. Nothing else
crosses an epoch: not walls, not granaries, not names except what the
Index holds. But matter crosses. Some settlement three epochs hence will
raise its wall out of yours, and this is not a morbid thought at a guild
supper; it is the closest thing the supper has to a grace. The formal
version appears in the Chronicle in a dozen hands across a dozen epochs,
always the same sentence: *we build with what they built, and we build
for whoever mines us.*

## The Quiet Shaft [DEEP]

Every few epochs, the record shows a vein that does not follow the
schedule — surfacing off-cadence, running past any seam's plausible
reserve, dry one survey and inexplicably yielding the next. The miners'
name for the pattern is the **Quiet Shaft**, and the sober explanation
is surveying error, and the sober explanation has a problem: assay.
Quiet Shaft ore runs heavy with FINISHED goods — not the Churn's crushed
and folded fragments but whole objects, unworn, sorted, as if CURATED.
Lamp-fittings, in every documented case. Lamp-fittings, wick-guides, and
small reflectors, in quantities no settlement ever manufactured, cached
in seams the Churn's timetable cannot account for. The historians file
the Quiet Shaft under anomalies. The ruin-divers, who have spent more
time in the strata than the historians, file it under something else,
and will say what over a drink but not into the Chronicle: the strata
are not entirely settled. Something down there is keeping stores.
Something down there is keeping stores OF LIGHT — and whatever it is
keeping them for, it has been keeping them a very, very long time.
